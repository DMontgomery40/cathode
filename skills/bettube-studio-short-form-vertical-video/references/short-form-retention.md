# Short-Form Retention Guide

Use this reference when writing or judging a 30-50 second short.

## Core Structure

| Time | Job |
| --- | --- |
| 0-2s | Scroll-stop visual or verbal hook. |
| 2-5s | Clarify the promise: what the viewer will learn, see, avoid, or get. |
| 5-12s | First proof, example, or partial payoff. |
| 12-28s | Two or three fast evidence/example beats. |
| 28-40s | Main payoff, reveal, answer, transformation, or punchline. |
| 40-50s | One CTA or loop-back ending. |

For 30 seconds, compress to hook, context, proof, payoff, CTA. For 50 seconds, add one extra evidence beat, not a second topic.

## Hook Taxonomy

Choose one hook type:

| Hook type | Pattern | Example shape |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Contradiction | Everyone thinks X, but Y. | "The fastest way to improve this is to stop adding more." |
| Mistake | You are doing X, but the problem is Y. | "Your intro is losing people before the topic starts." |
| Payoff-first | Show result before process. | "This 40-second clip came from a 12-minute video." |
| Curiosity gap | Reveal enough to create a specific missing piece. | "The trick is not the hook. It is what happens 7 seconds later." |
| Before/after | Contrast old state vs new state. | "This was a rambling tutorial. Now it is a clean short." |
| Stakes | Consequence of ignoring the idea. | "If the first line is generic, the rest barely matters." |
| Demonstration | Start mid-action. | "Watch what happens when I remove the first sentence." |
| Relatable POV | Put viewer inside the problem. | "You filmed a great answer, but the clip still feels slow." |

Good hooks create a concrete information gap. Bad hooks create vague mystery.

## Beat Rules

- 3-5 beats total.
- Each beat needs a verb: reveal, prove, contrast, simplify, demonstrate, escalate, or resolve.
- Do not restate the hook in different words.
- Do not add background unless the payoff becomes impossible without it.
- Deliver a partial payoff by 8-12 seconds so the viewer trusts the promise.
- Keep narration dense but intelligible. A 30-50 second short usually lands around 80-130 spoken words, depending on delivery speed and visuals.

## Pattern Interrupts

Plan a reset every 3-5 seconds:

- new shot, crop, or camera scale
- product/demo action
- before/after split
- caption emphasis
- B-roll matched to the spoken noun or verb
- fast example
- sound cue
- motion graphic
- prop or visual proof
- deliberate silence or pace shift

Use interrupts to advance meaning, not decorate.

## Captions

Captions are comprehension aids:

- Use short meaning chunks, often 3-7 words per card.
- Emphasize only the words carrying the idea.
- Avoid captioning filler words.
- Keep text high contrast, large, and inside safe zones.
- Never cover faces, product actions, proof, charts, or the visual punchline.
- Prefer one strong caption at a time over multiple competing labels.

## CTA

Use one CTA after the payoff:

- save this checklist
- comment with the source video
- watch the full breakdown
- try this on your next clip
- follow for the named series
- share with someone facing the problem

Do not ask for action before the viewer has received value unless the user is explicitly making an ad with an immediate offer.

## Anti-Clickbait Standard

Earned curiosity is allowed. Deception is not.

- Replace "You will not believe this" with the specific tension.
- Replace "This changes everything" with the actual changed thing.
- Never imply proof, authority, urgency, risk, or results the source does not support.
- Pay off the promise by the final third at the latest.
- Make the title, first frame, and first line describe the same promise.

## Evidence-Heavy Topics

For scientific, medical, legal, financial, or technical infrastructure subjects:

- Use a hook that is sharp but bounded.
- Include one beat that states what the evidence does and does not prove.
- Prefer "this makes X inspectable" over "this solves X" unless the source proves the stronger claim.
- Do not convert a modest benchmark, prototype, or transfer experiment into a solved-product claim.
- Let the constraint become part of the intrigue: "The signal is small, but it beats the right baseline" is often stronger than hype.
